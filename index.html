How Basic Valuation Works

This is a straightforward method ideal for small, owner-operated businesses. We calculate your Seller's Discretionary Earnings (SDE)—your total profit plus your salary and other one-time or non-operational expenses. This SDE is then multiplied by an industry-standard number to estimate your business's value.

Example Calculation:

First, we find your Pre-Tax Profit:
₹50L Revenue - ₹20L COGS - ₹15L OPEX = ₹15L Profit

Then, we calculate SDE by adding back non-operational or discretionary costs:
₹15L Profit + ₹12L Owner Salary + ₹2L Depreciation = ₹29L SDE

Finally, we apply the industry multiple:
₹29L SDE * 3.5x Multiple = ~₹1.01 Cr Valuation

How Intermediate Valuation Works

This method is common for larger businesses and is often used by investors. It uses EBITDA (Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization) as the earnings metric. We multiply your EBITDA by a baseline industry multiple, which is then adjusted based on your company's specific strengths and weaknesses to arrive at an Enterprise Value. We then adjust for debt and cash to find the final Equity Value.

Example Calculation:

First, we calculate the Enterprise Value:
₹25L EBITDA * 5.5x Adj. Multiple = ₹1.375 Cr Enterprise Value

Then, we adjust for debt and cash to find the Equity Value (what the business is worth to shareholders):
₹1.375 Cr EV - ₹10L Debt + ₹5L Cash = ₹1.325 Cr Equity Value

How Advanced Valuation Works

The Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) method is the most detailed, valuing a company based on its projected future cash flows. We forecast the company's free cash flow over the next 5 years, estimate a "terminal value" for all cash flows beyond that, and then discount all those future cash flows back to today's value using a discount rate that reflects the business's risk.

Example Free Cash Flow (FCF) Calculation (for one year):

1. Calculate Profit (EBIT): ₹1 Cr Revenue - ₹70L Costs = ₹30L EBIT
2. Calculate NOPAT (Net Operating Profit After Tax): ₹30L EBIT * (1 - 25% Tax) = ₹22.5L NOPAT
3. Calculate Free Cash Flow: ₹22.5L NOPAT + ₹4L D&A - ₹5L CapEx - ₹3L ΔNWC = ₹18.5L FCF

This FCF is calculated for each of the next 5 years. Each year's FCF is then "discounted" to find its value in today's money. The sum of these discounted cash flows gives the final valuation.

What is Business Valuation?

Business valuation is the process of determining the economic worth of a company. It's a critical exercise for owners, investors, and stakeholders, providing a quantifiable measure of a business's value for mergers, acquisitions, sales, fundraising, or strategic planning. The method used often depends on the company's size, industry, and maturity.

Key Terms for Basic Valuation

Seller's Discretionary Earnings (SDE)

The total financial benefit a single full-time owner-operator derives from the business. It's calculated by taking pre-tax profit and adding back the owner's salary, non-cash expenses (like depreciation), and any one-time or personal expenses run through the company.

Example: If a business has a ₹10,00,000 pre-tax profit, the owner draws a ₹12,00,000 salary, and there's ₹2,00,000 in depreciation, the SDE would be ₹24,00,000.

Formula: Pre-Tax Profit + Owner's Salary + Depreciation = ₹10,00,000 + ₹12,00,000 + ₹2,00,000 = ₹24,00,000

Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)

The direct costs attributable to the production of the goods or services sold by a company. This includes material costs and direct labor costs.

Example: For a coffee shop, COGS would include the cost of coffee beans, milk, sugar, and paper cups.

Operating Expenses (OPEX)

The expenses a business incurs through its normal business operations. These costs are not directly related to production and include things like rent, marketing, utilities, and administrative salaries.

Example: Monthly rent for an office, marketing campaign costs, and salaries for the sales team are all part of OPEX.

Key Terms for Intermediate Valuation

EBITDA

Stands for Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization. It's a measure of a company's overall financial performance and is used as an alternative to net income in some circumstances.

Example: A company with ₹50,00,000 in revenue, ₹20,00,000 in operating expenses, and ₹5,00,000 in depreciation would have an EBITDA of ₹30,00,000 (before considering interest and taxes).

Enterprise Value (EV)

A measure of a company's total value, often used as a more comprehensive alternative to equity market capitalization. EV is calculated as market capitalization plus debt, minus cash and cash equivalents.

Example: If a company's equity is valued at ₹2 Cr, it has ₹50L in debt and ₹20L in cash, its Enterprise Value is ₹2.3 Cr.

Formula: Equity Value + Debt - Cash = ₹2,00,00,000 + ₹50,00,000 - ₹20,00,000 = ₹2,30,00,000

Equity Value

The value of the company's shares and any other ownership interests. It represents the value remaining for shareholders after all debts and obligations have been paid.

Example: From the example above, if the Enterprise Value is ₹2.3 Cr, and you subtract debt (₹50L) and add back cash (₹20L), the Equity Value is ₹2 Cr.

Formula: Enterprise Value - Debt + Cash = ₹2,30,00,000 - ₹50,00,000 + ₹20,00,000 = ₹2,00,00,000

Key Terms for Advanced Valuation

Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)

A valuation method used to estimate the value of an investment based on its expected future cash flows. DCF analysis attempts to figure out the value of a company today, based on projections of how much money it will generate in the future.

Free Cash Flow (FCF)

The cash a company produces through its operations after subtracting for capital expenditures (CapEx). It's the cash left over after a company pays for its operating expenses and investments in assets.

Example: If a company has ₹1 Cr in cash from operations and invests ₹20L in new equipment (CapEx), its Free Cash Flow is ₹80L.

Formula: Cash from Operations - Capital Expenditures = ₹1,00,00,000 - ₹20,00,000 = ₹80,00,000

Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC)

A calculation of a firm's cost of capital in which each category of capital is proportionately weighted. It represents the blended cost of capital across all sources, including equity and debt.

Example: A high WACC (e.g., 20%) implies a higher risk and therefore a lower valuation, as future cash flows are discounted at a higher rate.

Terminal Value

The estimated value of a business for all the years beyond the explicit forecast period (usually 5 years). It assumes the business will grow at a set, stable rate forever.

Frequently Asked Questions

This calculator provides a data-driven, estimated valuation based on industry-standard methodologies and market data. It is an excellent starting point for strategic planning. However, it is not a substitute for a formal valuation conducted by a certified professional, which would involve a much deeper analysis of your financial statements, assets, and market position. For transactional purposes like selling a business or raising capital, we recommend consulting with a professional valuation expert.

Business valuation is not an exact science. The final price a business sells for can be influenced by many factors, including the negotiation process, the buyer's strategic goals, and the overall economic climate. Presenting the valuation as a range acknowledges these variables and provides a more realistic picture of the potential market value.

The choice depends on your business size and structure. Seller's Discretionary Earnings (SDE) is typically used for smaller, owner-operated businesses where the owner's salary is a significant portion of the profit. EBITDA is the standard for larger, more mature companies that have a professional management structure in place and would need to pay a market-rate salary to a CEO or general manager regardless of who owns the company.

Businesses at different stages of growth are valued differently. The Basic (SDE) method is best for owner-operated businesses. The Intermediate (EBITDA) method is for more mature, professionally managed companies. The Advanced (DCF) method is a forward-looking approach used by financial analysts to value a business based on its future cash flow potential.

An industry multiple is a market benchmark that reflects what similar businesses in your sector have recently sold for, relative to their earnings. These multiples are derived from data on thousands of private and public company transactions.
